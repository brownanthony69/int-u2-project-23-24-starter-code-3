The School to Prison Pipeline

How it Starts

The connection between prisons and schools in the United States is often referred to as the "school-to-prison pipeline." This term describes the various policies and practices within the education and criminal justice systems that contribute to a pathway where students, particularly those from marginalized communities, are funneled from schools into the criminal justice system. The school-to-prison pipeline is a disturbing societal phenomenon where the policies and practices within the education system contribute to the increased likelihood of students, particularly those from marginalized communities, becoming involved in the criminal justice system.

Who it Affects

The school-to-prison pipeline affects students of color, those with disabilities, and those from marginalized communities. It can lead to a lack of access to education, a lack of opportunities for learning and others. According to the US News the school-to-prison pipeline is a major issue in the United States. "The impacts are significantly more predictive for Black and Hispanic boys who attended strict middle schools. Black and Hispanic girls are more likely to be incarcerated than white girls."

Who is in Charge?

Several factors contribute to the development of the school-to-prison pipeline such as: Zero-tolerance policies: Many schools have implemented zero-tolerance policies for certain behaviors, leading to harsh and automatic punishment, often involving law enforcement. This can disproportionately affect students of color and those with disabilities. School discipline practices: Excessive use of suspensions, expulsions, and other punitive measures can contribute to students disengaging from school and, in some cases, becoming involved in the criminal justice system. Over-policing in schools: Some schools have a significant presence of law enforcement, leading to an increased likelihood of students being arrested for minor offenses. This can create a hostile environment and contribute to the criminalization of students. Socioeconomic factors: Students from low-income communities may face additional challenges, such as lack of access to quality education, economic instability, and limited resources, which can contribute to the likelihood of involvement in the criminal justice system. Racial disparities: There are significant racial disparities in the school-to-prison pipeline, with students of color, particularly African American and Latino students, experiencing higher rates of disciplinary actions and involvement in the criminal justice system.
